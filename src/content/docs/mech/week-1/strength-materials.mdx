## Load

It is the external force which acts on a body, machine parts or structural members. Its SI unit is Newton (N).

---

## Stress

The force of resistance per unit area, offered by a body against deformation is known as stress.

Mathematically stress is written as,

$$
\sigma = \frac{P}{A}
$$

Where:

- $\sigma$ = Stress (N/m² or N/mm²)  
- $P$ = Load (N)  
- $A$ = Area (m² or mm²)  

In the S.I. units, the force is expressed in newtons (written as N) and area is expressed as m². Hence unit of stress becomes N/m². The area is also expressed in millimetre square then unit of force becomes as N/mm².

---

## Strain

Then a body is subjected to some external force, there is some change of dimension of the body. The ratio of change of dimension of the body to the original dimension is known as strain. Strain is dimensionless.

$$
\text{Strain} = \frac{\text{Change in dimension}}{\text{Original dimension}}
$$

Strain may be:

### 1. Tensile strain
If there is some increase in length of a body due to external force, then the ratio of increase of length to the original length of the body is known as tensile strain.

Mathematically  

$$
\varepsilon_t = \frac{\Delta L}{L}
$$

### 2. Compressive strain
if there is some decrease in length of the body, then the ratio of decrease of the length of the body to the original length is known as compressive strain.

Mathematically  

$$
\varepsilon_c = \frac{\Delta L}{L}
$$

### 3. Volumetric strain
The ratio of change of volume of the body to the original volume is known as volumetric strain.

Mathematically  

$$
\varepsilon_v = \frac{\Delta V}{V}
$$

### 4. Shear strain
The strain produced by shear stress is known as shear strain.

Mathematically  

$$
\gamma = \tan \theta \approx \theta
$$

## TYPES OF STRESSES

There are two kinds of basic stresses which are:

- Normal stresses – always act normal to the stressed surface (either tensile or compressive)  
- Shearing stresses – always act parallel to the stressed surface  

---

The stress induced in a body, when subjected to two equal and opposite pulls  i result of which there is an increase in length, is known as tensile stress. The ratio of increase in length to known as tensile stress. The tensile stress act tensile strain. The tensile stress acts normal to the area and it pulls on the area  

Mathematically  

Tensile stress (σ) = Resisting force (R) / Cross-sectional area = Tensile load (P) / A  

$$
\sigma_t = \frac{P}{A}
$$

The stress induced in a body, when subjected to two equal and opposite pushes as shown in as a result of which there is a decrease in length of the body, is known as compressive stress.

## ANALYSIS OP BARS OF COMPOSITE SECTIONS

A composite bar is made up of two or more bars of equal length but different materials.  
These bars are rigidly fixed together and behave as a single unit under axial tensile or compressive loads.  

![Link](/Mech/mech1.png)

### Important Points:

- The extension or compression in each bar is the same.  
- Therefore, the strain (deformation per unit length) in each material is equal.  
- The total external load acting on the composite bar is equal to the sum of loads carried by each material.  

Let  

- $P$ = Total load on the composite bar  
- $L$ = Length of composite bar and also length of bars of different materials  
- $A_1$ = Area of cross-section of bar 1  
- $A_2$ = Area of cross-section of bar 2  
- $E_1$ = Young’s Modulus of bar 1  
- $E_2$ = Young’s Modulus of bar 2  
- $P_1$ = Load shared by bar 1  
- $P_2$ = Load shared by bar 2  
- $\sigma_1$ = Stress induced in bar 1  
- $\sigma_2$ = Stress induced in bar 2  

$$
P = P_1 + P_2
$$

Stress in bar 1 = Load carried by bar 1 / Area of cross-section of bar 1  

$$
\sigma_1 = \frac{P_1}{A_1} \quad \text{or} \quad P_1 = \sigma_1 A_1
$$

Similarly, stress in bar 2  

$$
\sigma_2 = \frac{P_2}{A_2} \quad \text{or} \quad P_2 = \sigma_2 A_2
$$

Substituting values  

$$
P = \sigma_1 A_1 + \sigma_2 A_2
$$

Strain in bar 1 = Stress in bar 1 / Young’s modulus of bar 1  

$$
\varepsilon_1 = \frac{\sigma_1}{E_1}
$$

Similarly, strain in bar 2  

$$
\varepsilon_2 = \frac{\sigma_2}{E_2}
$$

## Hooke’s law

Hooke’s Law states that when a material is loaded within elastic limit, the stress is proportional to the strain produced by the stress. This means the ratio of the stress to the corresponding strain is a constant within the elastic limit. This constant is known as Modulus of Elasticity or Modulus of Rigidity  

$$
\frac{\text{Normal stress}}{\text{Corresponding strain}} = \text{Constant}
$$

---

## Elastic constants

### Young’s Modulus

The ratio of tensile stress or compressive stress to the corresponding strain is a constant. This ratio is known as Young’s Modulus or Modulus of Elasticity.  

Mathematically  

$$
E = \frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon}
$$

---

### Shear Modulus

The ratio of shear stress to the corresponding shear strain within the elastic limit, is known as Modulus of Rigidity.  

Mathematically  

$$
G = \frac{\tau}{\gamma}
$$

---

### Bulk Modulus
Bulk Modulus is the ratio of volumetric stress to the corresponding volumetric strain within the elastic limit.
It measures a material’s resistance to uniform compression (change in volume).

Mathematically  

$$
K = \frac{\text{Volumetric stress}}{\text{Volumetric strain}}
$$

$$
K = \frac{p}{\Delta V / V}
$$

## RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN STRESS AND STRAIN

### For One-Dimensional Stress System

The relationship between stress and strain for a unidirectional stress (i.e., for normal stress in one direction only) is given by Hooke’s law.  

Mathematically  

$$
\frac{\text{Normal stress}}{\text{Corresponding strain}} = \text{Constant}
$$

---

### For Two-Dimensional Stress System

#### Longitudinal strain

Longitudinal strain. When a body is subjected to an axial tensile load, there is an increase in the length [of the body. But at the same time there is a decrease in other dimensions of the body at right singles to the line of action of the applied load. Thus the body is laying axial deformation an(| also deformation at right angles to the line of action of the applied load|

Mathematically  

$$
\varepsilon_1 = \frac{\sigma_1}{E} - \mu \left(\frac{\sigma_2}{E}\right)
$$

$$
\varepsilon_2 = \frac{\sigma_2}{E} - \mu \left(\frac{\sigma_1}{E}\right)
$$

Where  

- $\varepsilon_1, \varepsilon_2$ = Longitudinal strains in two directions  
- $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ = Stresses in two perpendicular directions  
- $E$ = Young’s Modulus  
- $\mu$ = Poisson’s ratio  

---

### Lateral strain

The strain at right angles to the direction of applied load is known as lateral strain.  

Mathematically  

$$
\text{Lateral strain} = \frac{\text{Change in lateral dimension}}{\text{Original lateral dimension}}
$$

$$
\varepsilon_{lat} = \frac{\Delta d}{d}
$$

Using Poisson’s Ratio  

$$
\varepsilon_{lat} = - \mu \times \varepsilon_{long}
$$

---

### Poisson’s ratio

The ratio of lateral strain to the longitudinal strain is a constant for a given material, when the material is stressed within the elastic limit.  

$$
\mu = \frac{\text{Lateral strain}}{\text{Longitudinal strain}}
$$

## Principle of Superposition

The principle of superposition states that when multiple forces act on an elastic body, the total deformation (strain) is equal to the sum of the deformations caused by each force acting individually.  

$$
\text{Total deformation} = \delta_1 + \delta_2 + \delta_3 + \dots
$$

To understand this best, imagine a horizontal metal rod fixed at one end, divided into three sections (A, B, and C), with different forces pulling and pushing at various points along its length.  

Imagine a rod where  

- Section 1 is being pulled by 100 N.  
- Section 2 is being pushed by 50 N.  
- Section 3 is being pulled by 20 N.  

### Step-by-Step Example

Analyze Section 1  
We look only at the first part of the rod. Based on the forces, it feels a total pulling force (tension). We calculate its change in length, which might be +-0.5mm.  

Analyze Section 2  
Because of the 50 N pushing force, this middle part of the rod is being compressed. We calculate its change, which might be -0.2mm  

Analyze Section 3  
The final section has a light pulling force. Its change is calculated as +0.1mm.  

### The Superposition Math

Instead of trying to guess how the whole rod moves at once, we just add our results  

$$
(+0.5 \text{ mm}) + (-0.2 \text{ mm}) + (+0.1 \text{ mm}) = +0.4 \text{ mm}
$$

### The Final Result

The entire rod has increased in length by 0.4 mm.

## Principal Plane

The planes, which have no shear stress are known as principal planes. Hence principal planes are the planes of zero shear stress. These planes carry only normal stresses. The normal stresses acting on a principal plane, are known as principal stresses  

---

## Principal Stress

These are the purely normal stresses (either tensile or compressive) that act upon the principal planes.  

---

## Case I: Member Subjected to Direct Stress in One Plane

Definition: A rectangular member of uniform area A and unit thickness subjected to an axial force P along the X-axis.  

$$
\sigma_n = \sigma \cos^2 \theta
$$

$$
\tau = \frac{\sigma}{2} \sin 2\theta
$$

Maximum normal stress occurs at $\theta = 0^\circ$, value = $\sigma$  

Maximum shear stress occurs at $\theta = 45^\circ$ or $135^\circ$, value = $\frac{\sigma}{2}$  

---

## Case II: Member Subjected to Two Perpendicular Stresses

Definition: A bar subjected to two direct tensile stresses $\sigma_1$ (major) and $\sigma_2$ (minor).  

$$
\sigma_n = \frac{\sigma_1 + \sigma_2}{2} + \frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2} \cos 2\theta
$$

$$
\tau = \frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2} \sin 2\theta
$$

Maximum shear stress occurs when $\sin 2\theta = 1$ $(\theta = 45^\circ \text{ or } 135^\circ)$  

$$
\tau_{max} = \frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2}
$$

---

## Case III: Member Subjected to Simple Shear Stress

Definition: A bar subjected to simple shear stress $\tau$.  

$$
\sigma_n = \tau \sin 2\theta
$$

$$
\sigma_n = \tau \sin 2\theta
$$

---

## Equations for Principal Stresses and Planes

### Position of Principal Planes

The angles at which the shear stress is zero.  

$$
\tan 2\theta = \frac{2\tau}{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}
$$

---

### Major Principal Stress

The maximum normal stress acting on a principal plane.  

$$
\sigma_{major} = \frac{\sigma_1 + \sigma_2}{2} + \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2}\right)^2 + \tau^2}
$$

---

### Minor Principal Stress

The minimum normal stress acting on a principal plane.  

$$
\sigma_{minor} = \frac{\sigma_1 + \sigma_2}{2} - \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2}\right)^2 + \tau^2}
$$

---

### Maximum Shear Stress

The greatest intensity of shear stress within the material.  

$$
\tau_{max} = \frac{1}{2} \sqrt{(\sigma_1 - \sigma_2)^2 + 4\tau^2}
$$

## Mohr’s Circle for Plane Stress

The transformation equations for plane stress can be represented in a graphical format known as Mohr’s circle. This representation is useful in visualizing the relationships between normal and shear stresses acting on various inclined planes at a point in a stressed body.

---

## Rules for construction of Mohr’s circle

### Center

$$
C = \frac{\sigma_x + \sigma_y}{2}
$$

### Radius

$$
R = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\sigma_x - \sigma_y}{2}\right)^2 + \tau_{xy}^2}
$$

---

| **Stress Component** | **Plot** |
|---------------------|----------|
| Normal Stresses (Horizontal Axis) | + For Tension <br /> - For Compression |
| Shear Stresses (Vertical Axis) | + For Clockwise Shear <br /> - For Counterclockwise Shear |

---

## Construct Mohr’s circle as follows

1. Determine the point on the body in which the principal stresses are to be determined.  
2. Treating the load cases independently and calculated the stresses for the point chosen.  
3. Choose a set of x-y reference axes and draw a square element centered on the axes.  
4. Identify the stresses $\sigma_x$, $\sigma_y$, and $\tau_{xy} = \tau_{yx}$ and list them with the proper sign.  
5. Draw a set of $\sigma - \tau$ coordinate axes with $\sigma$ being positive to the right and $\tau$ being positive in the upward direction. Choose an appropriate scale for the each axis.  
6. Using the rules on the previous page, plot the stresses on the x face of the element in this coordinate system (point V). Repeat the process for the y face (point H).  
7. Draw a line between the two point V and H. The point where this line crosses the $\sigma$ axis establishes the center of the circle.  
8. Draw the complete circle.  
9. The line from the center of the circle to point V identifies the x axis or reference axis for angle measurements (i.e. $\theta = 0$).  

---

Note: The angle between the reference axis and the $\sigma$ axis is equal to $2\theta_p$.

## Beam

A beam is usually a horizontal structural member that is subjected to a load that tends to cause it to bend,

---

## Types of Beams

### Based on Support Conditions

- **Simply Supported Beam:** A beam that rests freely on two supports, usually at its ends  

- **Cantilever Beam:** A beam that is rigidly fixed at one end and completely free at the other end  

- **Overhanging Beam:** A beam where the end portion extends beyond the support  
  This can be a single overhang (extending past one support) or a double overhang (extending past both supports)  

- **Fixed Beam:** A beam where both ends are rigidly fixed or built into walls  
  These are also known as built-in or encastred beams  
  They are generally stiffer, stronger, and more stable than simply supported beams  

- **Continuous Beam:** A beam that is placed on more than two supports  
  Engineers use these when the span of the beam is very large, ensuring that the deflection under each rigid support is zero  

---

### Based on Analysis

Types of Beams  

From an engineering analysis perspective, beams are also divided into two categories based on how you calculate their forces:

- **Statically Determinate Beams:** Beams where you can find all the reaction forces and internal forces using only the basic equations of static equilibrium  

- **Statically Indeterminate Beams:** Beams where the static equilibrium equations alone are not enough to find all the unknown forces  
  To solve these, an engineer must use additional deformation equations  
  Fixed beams and continuous beams typically fall into this category  

---

## Shear Force & Bending Moment

### Shear force

At any given point on a beam, the shear force is the total algebraic sum of all the vertical forces (loads pushing down and supports pushing up) acting on one side of that point.  

What it does: It is the internal resistance that stops the left side of the beam from sliding vertically past the right side  

---

### Bending Moment

At any point on a beam, the bending moment is the total sum of the rotational forces (moments) acting on one side of that point  

It is the internal resistance that stops the beam from simply snapping or folding under the weight  

---

## Sign Convention

A bending moment is classified as either positive or negative based on how it tends to bend or curve the beam:

---

### 1. Positive Bending Moment (Sagging Moment)

- The "Smile" Rule: A bending moment is considered positive if it causes the beam to bend so that there is concavity at the top (curving upwards into a "smile" shape)  

- Engineers commonly refer to this as a sagging moment  

- The Directional Rule: If you conceptually cut a section of the beam, the bending moment is positive if the net rotational force on the left side of the cut is clockwise, and the net rotational force on the right side is anti-clockwise  

---

### 2. Negative Bending Moment (Hogging Moment)

- The "Frown" Rule: A bending moment is considered negative if it causes the beam to bend so that there is convexity at the top (curving downwards into a "frown" shape) This is known as a hogging moment  


- The Directional Rule: The bending moment is negative when the net rotational force on the left side of the cut is anti-clockwise, and on the right side is clockwise.
