An amplifier is an electronic circuit or device used to increase the amplitude, voltage, current, or power level of an input signal without changing the basic shape of the signal. Amplifiers are widely used in communication systems, audio systems, radio transmitters, television circuits, and signal processing applications. The main purpose of an amplifier is to take a weak signal and produce a stronger version of the same signal at the output.

The input signal applied to the amplifier is generally very weak. The amplifier uses energy from a DC power supply and transfers it to the input signal, thereby increasing the signal strength at the output.

Amplifiers are mainly used with analogue signals, although digital amplification is also possible in certain applications.

## TRANSISTOR AS AN AMPLIFIER

Transistors such as:

- BJT
- JFET
- MOSFET

can be used as amplifiers.

For proper amplification, the transistor must operate in the active region. Therefore, suitable DC biasing must be provided.

## 1. GAIN OF AMPLIFIER

Gain represents the amount of amplification provided by the amplifier.

Gain is the ratio of output quantity to input quantity.

### 1.a VOLTAGE GAIN

Voltage gain is the ratio of output voltage to input voltage.

$$
A_v = \frac{V_{out}}{V_{in}}
$$

If $V_{out} > V_{in}$, then amplification occurs.

### 1.b CURRENT GAIN

Current gain is the ratio of output current to input current.

$$
A_i = \frac{I_{out}}{I_{in}}
$$

For transistor:

$$
A_i = \frac{I_C}{I_B} = \beta
$$

### 1.c POWER GAIN

Power gain is the ratio of output power to input power.

$$
A_p = \frac{P_{out}}{P_{in}}
$$

Since $P = VI$, power gain can also be written as:

$$
A_p = A_v \times A_i
$$

Thus power gain equals product of voltage gain and current gain.

---

## INPUT IMPEDANCE

Input impedance is the opposition offered by the amplifier to the input signal.

$$
Z_{in} = \frac{V_{in}}{I_{in}}
$$

A high input impedance is desirable because:

- Amplifier draws less current from source
- Signal loss becomes smaller

## OUTPUT IMPEDANCE

Output impedance is the opposition offered by the amplifier at the output terminal.

$$
Z_{out} = \frac{V_{out}}{I_{out}}
$$

A low output impedance is desirable because:

- More power is transferred to load
- Output voltage drop becomes smaller

## 2. CLASSIFICATION OF AMPLIFIERS

Amplifiers are classified based on signal level and mode of operation.

### 2.a SMALL SIGNAL AMPLIFIERS / VOLTAGE AMPLIFIERS

These amplifiers are designed to amplify small input signals.

**Main objective:** Increase voltage amplitude.

**Characteristics:**

- Small output power
- High voltage gain
- Used in preamplifier stages

### 2.b LARGE SIGNAL AMPLIFIERS / POWER AMPLIFIERS

Power amplifiers amplify large signals and deliver high power to the load.

These amplifiers convert DC power from supply into AC power at output.

Power transistors are generally used because large currents are involved.

In Class A operation, the transistor conducts for the entire $360^\circ$ of input cycle.

**Conduction angle:** $360^\circ$

### WORKING

The transistor remains in active region throughout the complete cycle. Therefore:

- Entire signal gets amplified
- Output distortion is very low

### ADVANTAGES

- Excellent signal quality
- Very low distortion

### DISADVANTAGES

- Very low efficiency
- Large power loss as heat

**Maximum efficiency:** $\approx 25\%$ to $30\%$

In Class B operation, each transistor conducts for only half cycle of input signal.

**Conduction angle:** $180^\circ$

### WORKING

One transistor amplifies positive half cycle. Another complementary transistor amplifies negative half cycle. Both outputs combine to produce complete waveform.

### ADVANTAGES

- Higher efficiency than Class A
- Less power loss

### DISADVANTAGES

- Crossover distortion occurs near zero crossing

Near zero voltage:

- Neither transistor conducts immediately
- Small dead region appears
- Output waveform becomes distorted

**Maximum efficiency:** $\approx 78.5\%$

## 6. FEEDBACK AMPLIFIER

A feedback amplifier is an electronic circuit in which a portion of the output signal is returned back to the input. This feedback helps control the amplifier's performance and improves stability, gain control, bandwidth, and distortion characteristics.

In a feedback system:

- Output is sampled
- A fraction is fed back to input using feedback factor $\beta$
- Input is modified based on feedback signal

### BASIC WORKING RELATION

Feedback signal:

$$
V_f = \beta V_o
$$

**Negative feedback** — input signal with feedback:

$$
V_i = V_s - \beta V_o
$$

**Positive feedback** — input signal with feedback:

$$
V_i = V_s + \beta V_o
$$

Amplifier output:

$$
V_o = A \times V_i
$$

**Substituting for Negative feedback:**

$$
V_o = A(V_s - \beta V_o)
$$

$$
V_o = AV_s - A\beta V_o
$$

$$
V_o(1 + A\beta) = AV_s
$$

**Closed-loop gain (Negative feedback):**

$$
A_f = \frac{V_o}{V_s} = \frac{A}{1 + A\beta}
$$

**Substituting for Positive feedback:**

$$
V_o = A(V_s + \beta V_o)
$$

$$
V_o = AV_s + A\beta V_o
$$

$$
V_o(1 - A\beta) = AV_s
$$

**Closed-loop gain (Positive feedback):**

$$
A_f = \frac{A}{1 - A\beta}
$$

### UNITY FEEDBACK

Unity feedback means $\beta = 1$.

Negative feedback:

$$
A_f = \frac{A}{1 + A}
$$

Positive feedback:

$$
A_f = \frac{A}{1 - A}
$$

---

### FEEDBACK TYPES

#### 1. POSITIVE FEEDBACK

Feedback signal is in phase with input, so it adds to input signal.

**Effects:**

- Increases gain
- Reduces stability
- Can lead to oscillations
- Used in oscillators and signal regeneration

**Condition for oscillation:**

$$
A\beta = 1
$$

(with correct phase condition)

#### 2. NEGATIVE FEEDBACK

Feedback signal is opposite in phase to input, so it subtracts from input signal.

**Effects:**

- Reduces gain
- Improves stability
- Reduces distortion and noise
- Increases bandwidth
- Improves linearity

---

**Gain:**

$$
A_f = \frac{A}{1 + A\beta}
$$

Gain decreases but becomes stable.

**Bandwidth:**

$$
BW_f = BW(1 + A\beta)
$$

Bandwidth increases with feedback.

**Distortion:**

$$
D_f = \frac{D}{1 + A\beta}
$$

Distortion reduces significantly.

**Noise:**

$$
N_f = \frac{N}{1 + A\beta}
$$

Noise gets reduced.

**Stability:** Feedback reduces sensitivity to transistor parameter changes (temperature, aging), so stability improves.

## 7. OPERATIONAL AMPLIFIER (OP-AMP) BASICS

An operational amplifier (op-amp) is a high-gain electronic voltage amplifier with two inputs and one output. It is mainly used with external feedback components like resistors and capacitors to perform mathematical and signal-processing operations such as amplification, addition, subtraction, integration, and differentiation. The key idea is that the op-amp itself has very high gain but we control it using feedback so that it behaves in a stable and predictable way in real circuits.

### BASIC STRUCTURE

<img
  src="https://raw.githubusercontent.com/podummy04/images/main/EEE/analogelectronics/week3/AE8.png"
  alt="AE1"
  style={{
    width: "65%",
    display: "block",
    margin: "20px auto"
  }}
/>

An op-amp has three terminals:

1. Inverting input ($-$)
2. Non-inverting input ($+$)
3. Output

The output depends on the difference between the two input voltages:

$$
V_{out} = A(V_{+} - V_{-})
$$

Where $A$ = open-loop gain (very large in ideal case).

The op-amp does not amplify each input separately. Instead, it amplifies only the difference between the two inputs. Because the open-loop gain is extremely high, even a very small difference between the inputs can drive the output to saturation if feedback is not used.

### WORKING PRINCIPLE

The op-amp amplifies only the difference between the two input signals.

- If $V_{+} = V_{-}$ → Output $= 0$ (ideal case)
- If $V_{+} > V_{-}$ → Positive output
- If $V_{+} < V_{-}$ → Negative output

So, op-amp behaves as a differential amplifier.

---

### IDEAL OP-AMP CHARACTERISTICS

**1. Infinite open-loop gain:**

$$
A \rightarrow \infty
$$

This means even a tiny input difference is enough to produce a full-scale output. In real life, this gain is very large but not infinite, typically around $10^5$ to $10^6$.

**2. Infinite input impedance:**

$$
Z_{in} \rightarrow \infty
$$

(no input current flows) — The op-amp does not draw current from the signal source. This prevents loading effect and ensures the input signal is not disturbed.

**3. Zero output impedance:**

$$
Z_{out} \rightarrow 0
$$

(acts as ideal voltage source) — The op-amp can drive any load without losing output voltage. In real op-amps, output impedance is low but not zero.

**4. Infinite bandwidth:** Can amplify all frequencies — an ideal op-amp would amplify DC to infinite frequency equally. In reality, gain decreases as frequency increases due to internal limitations.

**5. Zero offset voltage:** Output is zero when inputs are equal. There is no unwanted output when both inputs are at the same potential. Practical op-amps show small offset due to internal mismatches.

---

### 1. VIRTUAL SHORT

Due to very high gain and negative feedback:

$$
V_{+} \approx V_{-}
$$

This does NOT mean the terminals are physically connected. It only means the circuit forces both input voltages to be almost equal. This happens because any tiny difference is immediately corrected by the high gain output response.

### 2. VIRTUAL GROUND

If one input is grounded and feedback is used, that node behaves like ground ($0\,V$) even though not physically connected. Feedback forces the node to maintain nearly zero voltage. Even if current flows into that node, the voltage remains at approximately zero due to op-amp action.

### 3. GAIN WITH NEGATIVE FEEDBACK

Open-loop gain is very high, so feedback controls final gain. Closed-loop gain becomes stable and predictable because it depends mainly on external resistors. This removes dependency on internal transistor parameters.

### 4. OP-AMP FREQUENCY BEHAVIOR

Gain decreases as frequency increases.

**Gain-Bandwidth Product (GBP):**

$$
GBP = \text{Gain} \times \text{Bandwidth}
$$

This is constant for a given op-amp. At low frequency, the op-amp gives high gain, but as frequency increases, internal capacitances start limiting the response. This is why high-gain amplifier circuits have lower bandwidth.

# B. OSCILLATORS

## BASIC PRINCIPLE OF OSCILLATION

An oscillator is basically an amplifier with positive feedback.

**Working idea:**

- Noise or small disturbance starts the signal
- Positive feedback reinforces it
- Signal keeps sustaining itself

**Condition:** The circuit must return a portion of output back to input in correct phase and magnitude.

---

## BARKHAUSEN CRITERION

For sustained oscillations in a feedback system:

1. $|A\beta| = 1$
2. Total phase shift $= 0^\circ$ or $360^\circ$

This means the signal fed back to the input must be exactly equal in magnitude and in correct phase so that it reinforces itself continuously. If the loop gain is less than $1$, oscillations die out due to loss. If it is greater than $1$, oscillations grow and become distorted or unstable. This condition is essential in oscillator design where we intentionally create continuous wave generation without external input.

---

## FREQUENCY OF OSCILLATION

The frequency of oscillation is the frequency at which the circuit naturally produces output.

It depends on:

- Resistors ($R$)
- Capacitors ($C$)
- Inductors ($L$)
- Feedback network design

Each oscillator type has its own frequency formula based on its circuit configuration.

---

**Condition:** Gain must be exactly $3$ for sustained oscillation.

**Use:** Audio frequency generation and testing instruments

# Problems

## 1. Negative Feedback Amplifier — Loop Gain and Closed-Loop Gain

A negative feedback amplifier has an open-loop gain of 500 and a feedback factor of 0.02. Calculate the loop gain and the closed-loop gain of the amplifier.

**Given:**

$$
A = 500, \quad \beta = 0.02
$$

**Step 1: Loop Gain**

$$
A\beta = A \times \beta = 500 \times 0.02 = 10
$$

$$\boxed{A\beta = 10}$$

**Step 2: Closed-Loop Gain**

$$
A_f = \frac{A}{1 + A\beta} = \frac{500}{1 + 10} = \frac{500}{11}
$$

$$\boxed{A_f = 45.45}$$

---

## 2. Inverting Op-Amp — Voltage Gain and Output Voltage

An inverting operational amplifier circuit uses an input resistor of $5\,k\Omega$ and a feedback resistor of $100\,k\Omega$. If the input voltage is $0.2\,V$, calculate the voltage gain and output voltage.

**Given:**

$$
R_{in} = 5\,k\Omega, \quad R_f = 100\,k\Omega, \quad V_{in} = 0.2\,V
$$

**Step 1: Calculate Voltage Gain**

$$
A_v = -\left(\frac{R_f}{R_{in}}\right) = -\left(\frac{100k}{5k}\right) = -20
$$

$$\boxed{A_v = -20}$$

**Step 2: Calculate Output Voltage**

$$
V_{out} = A_v \times V_{in} = -20 \times 0.2
$$

$$\boxed{V_{out} = -4\,V}$$

---

## 3. Colpitts Oscillator — Equivalent Capacitance and Frequency

A Colpitts oscillator uses an inductor of $20\,\mu H$ and two capacitors of $100\,pF$ and $400\,pF$. Calculate the equivalent capacitance and the frequency of oscillation.

**Given:**

$$
L = 20\,\mu H = 20 \times 10^{-6}\,H
$$

$$
C_1 = 100\,pF = 100 \times 10^{-12}\,F, \quad C_2 = 400\,pF = 400 \times 10^{-12}\,F
$$

**Step 1: Equivalent Capacitance**

$$
C_{eq} = \frac{C_1 \times C_2}{C_1 + C_2} = \frac{100 \times 400}{100 + 400}\,pF = \frac{40000}{500}
$$

$$\boxed{C_{eq} = 80\,pF = 80 \times 10^{-12}\,F}$$
