# Power Electronics

Power electronics is the study of conversion and control of electrical power using semiconductor devices. It is used to efficiently convert power from one form to another (AC ↔ DC, DC ↔ DC, AC ↔ AC).

---

## Steps

1. Input power supply (AC/DC)
2. Use power semiconductor device
3. Control switching (ON/OFF)
4. Get desired output (controlled voltage/current)

---

## Types of Power Conversion

- **AC to DC** → Rectifier
- **DC to AC** → Inverter
- **DC to DC** → Chopper
- **AC to AC** → Cycloconverter / AC Controller

---

## Reverse Recovery Time ($t_{rr}$)

Time taken by diode to switch from forward conducting to reverse blocking mode.

### Important Points

- Affects efficiency and heat dissipation
- Important in high-frequency applications

---

# Thyristor Characteristics

A thyristor is a 4-layer (PNPN) semiconductor device acting as a high-power, unidirectional bistable switch with anode, cathode, and gate terminals.

### Key Characteristics

- Forward Blocking
- Reverse Blocking
- Forward Conduction

It can handle:

$$
>12\text{kV}
$$

and currents:

$$
>6\text{kA}
$$

Once triggered by a positive gate pulse, it stays conductive until the anode current drops below the holding current.

---

## Static Characteristics

Describes the V-I behavior during ON and OFF states.

- Forward Blocking
- Forward Conduction
- Reverse Blocking

---

## Dynamic Characteristics

- Turn-On Time
- Turn-Off Time
- Reverse Recovery

---

## Special Case

Devices operate in switching mode (ON/OFF), reducing power loss compared to linear devices.

---

## Important Points

- Power electronics deals with high voltage and current
- Devices act as switches, not amplifiers
- SCR cannot turn OFF by gate
- MOSFET provides fast switching
- IGBT has high power capability

---

## Limitations

- Switching losses
- Harmonics in output
- Requires proper cooling

---

# Power Semiconductor Devices

# 1. Diode

## Definition

A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device that allows current to flow in one direction only and blocks it in the opposite direction.

---

## Symbol & Terminals

- Anode (A) → Positive terminal
- Cathode (K) → Negative terminal
- Current flows from Anode → Cathode

---

## Working Principle

A diode is made from a PN junction:

- P-side → holes
- N-side → electrons

At the junction:

- A depletion region forms
- Acts as a barrier to current flow

---

## Biasing of Diode

### (a) Forward Bias

- P-side connected to +ve
- N-side connected to –ve
- Depletion layer decreases
- Current flows
- Diode acts like a closed switch

### (b) Reverse Bias

- P-side connected to –ve
- N-side connected to +ve
- Depletion layer increases
- No current flows except leakage current
- Diode acts like an open switch

---

## V-I Characteristics

![PE Image 1](./PE%20Image%201.png)

### Forward Bias

Current increases rapidly after threshold voltage.

Threshold voltage:

- Silicon diode:

$$
0.7\text{V}
$$

- Germanium diode:

$$
0.3\text{V}
$$

### Reverse Bias

- Very small reverse saturation current
- Sudden increase at breakdown voltage

---

## Types of Diodes

### 1. PN Junction Diode

Basic diode used for rectification.

A PN junction diode is formed by joining p-type and n-type semiconductor materials.

---

### 2. Zener Diode

A Zener diode operates in reverse breakdown region for voltage regulation.

Maintains constant voltage:

$$
V_Z
$$

Operating range:

$$
2.4\text{V} \text{ to } 200\text{V}
$$

#### Uses

- Voltage regulation
- Surge protection

---

### 3. LED (Light Emitting Diode)

A semiconductor device that emits light when forward biased.

#### Applications

- Displays
- Indicators
- Lighting

---

### 4. Photodiode

A photodiode converts light into electrical current.

#### Features

- Operates in reverse bias
- Detects UV, visible, and infrared light

---

### 5. Schottky Diode

A metal-semiconductor junction diode with:

- Low forward voltage drop
- Fast switching speed

Forward voltage drop:

$$
0.15\text{V} - 0.45\text{V}
$$

---

## Applications of Diodes

- Rectifiers
- Clippers and clampers
- Voltage regulators
- Signal demodulation
- Protection circuits

---

# 2. SCR (Silicon Controlled Rectifier)

## Definition

An SCR is a three-terminal semiconductor device used as a controlled switch.

---

## Terminals

- Anode (A)
- Cathode (K)
- Gate (G)

Current flows from Anode → Cathode when triggered.

---

## Construction

- Four-layer PNPN structure
- Junctions:

$$
J_1,\ J_2,\ J_3
$$

---

## Working Principle

### (a) Forward Blocking Mode

- Anode positive
- Cathode negative
- SCR OFF

### (b) Forward Conduction Mode

- Gate pulse applied
- SCR turns ON
- Remains ON after gate pulse removal

### (c) Reverse Blocking Mode

- Anode negative
- Cathode positive
- No conduction

---

## V-I Characteristics

![PE Image 2](./PE%20Image%202.png)

SCR initially remains OFF in forward bias and turns ON by:

- Gate triggering
- Breakover voltage

---

## Important Terms

### Latching Current ($I_L$)

Minimum current required to keep SCR ON after triggering.

### Holding Current ($I_H$)

Minimum current below which SCR turns OFF.

$$
I_L > I_H
$$

---

## Switching Action

- Turn ON → Gate pulse
- Turn OFF → Reduce current below holding current

---

## Two-Transistor Analogy

SCR can be represented as:

- One PNP transistor
- One NPN transistor

Positive feedback enables rapid switching.

---

## Advantages

- High efficiency
- High voltage/current handling
- Fast switching
- Compact

---

## Applications

- Controlled rectifiers
- Light dimmers
- Motor speed control
- Power control circuits
- Inverters and converters

---

## Key Points

- Unidirectional controlled device
- Requires gate signal to turn ON
- Cannot turn OFF by gate

---

# 3. MOSFET

## Definition

A MOSFET is a voltage-controlled semiconductor device used for switching and amplification.

---

## Terminals

- Gate (G)
- Drain (D)
- Source (S)

Current flows from Drain → Source.

---

## Construction

- Semiconductor substrate
- Gate insulated using:

$$
SiO_2
$$

- Very high input impedance

---

## Types of MOSFET

### (a) Enhancement MOSFET

- Normally OFF
- Requires gate voltage to turn ON

### (b) Depletion MOSFET

- Normally ON
- Can be turned OFF using gate voltage

---

## Channel Types

- N-channel MOSFET
- P-channel MOSFET

N-channel MOSFET is more efficient.

---

## Working Principle

### Case 1

$$
V_{GS}=0
$$

- No channel formed
- No current flow

### Case 2

$$
V_{GS} > V_T
$$

- Channel forms
- Current flows

---

## Regions of Operation

### 1. Cut-off Region

$$
V_{GS} < V_T
$$

Device OFF.

### 2. Linear Region

- Acts like resistor
- Current increases linearly

### 3. Saturation Region

- Acts like switch/amplifier
- Current becomes constant

---

## Characteristics

![PE Image 3](./PE%20Image%203.png)

### Output Characteristics

$$
I_D \text{ vs } V_{DS}
$$

### Transfer Characteristics

$$
I_D \text{ vs } V_{GS}
$$

---

## Advantages

- Very high input impedance
- Low power consumption
- Fast switching
- Small size

---

## Applications

- CMOS technology
- Amplifiers
- Switching devices
- Power electronics
- Microprocessors

---

## Key Points

- Voltage-controlled device
- Gate insulated
- Widely used in ICs

---

# 4. IGBT (Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistor)

## Definition

An IGBT combines:

- MOSFET’s high input impedance
- BJT’s high current capability

Used in power electronics for switching and control.

---

## Terminals

- Gate (G)
- Collector (C)
- Emitter (E)

Current flows from Collector → Emitter.

---

## Construction

- MOSFET input structure
- BJT output structure
- PNPN layers

---

## Working Principle

### OFF State

$$
V_{GE}=0
$$

- No conduction
- Open switch

### ON State

- Positive gate voltage applied
- Channel forms
- Heavy current conduction

---

## Characteristics

![PE Image 4](./PE%20Image%204.png)

### Features

- High voltage capability
- Low conduction losses
- Moderate switching speed

---

## Advantages

- High input impedance
- Low gate power
- High efficiency
- High voltage/current handling

---

## Disadvantages

- Slower than MOSFET
- Latch-up issue
- Costlier than BJT

---

## Applications

- Inverters
- Motor drives
- UPS systems
- Electric vehicles
- Power converters
- Induction heating

---

# Comparison: IGBT vs MOSFET vs BJT

| Feature | IGBT | MOSFET | BJT |
|---|---|---|---|
| Control | Voltage | Voltage | Current |
| Speed | Medium | High | Low |
| Power Handling | High | Medium | High |
| Input Impedance | High | Very High | Low |

# Rectifier

A rectifier converts AC into DC using diodes.

![PE Image 5](./PE%20Image%205.png)

---

## Types of Rectifiers

- Half Wave Rectifier (HWR)
- Full Wave Rectifier (FWR)
  - Center-tapped
  - Bridge rectifier

---

## Working Steps

1. Apply AC input
2. Diode conducts in one direction
3. Output becomes pulsating DC
4. Filter smoothens output

# Comparison of Rectifiers

| Feature | Half-Wave | Full-Wave (CT) | Bridge |
|---|---|---|---|
| Number of Diodes | 1 | 2 | 4 |

## Ripple Factor

Ripple factor is:

$$
r=\sqrt{\left(\frac{V_{rms}}{V_{dc}}\right)^2-1}
$$

# Inverter

An inverter converts DC into AC.

Applications:

- UPS
- Solar systems
- Motor drives

---

## Types of Inverters

- Single-phase
- Three-phase
- VSI
- CSI

---

## Working Steps

1. Input DC supply
2. Use MOSFET/IGBT
3. Switch ON/OFF
4. Produce AC waveform

---

## Important Equations

### Output Voltage

$$
V_o = \pm V_{dc}
$$

### RMS Output Voltage

$$
V_{rms}=V_{dc}
$$

### Frequency

$$
f=\frac{1}{T}
$$

# Chopper (DC–DC Converter)

A chopper converts fixed DC into variable DC voltage.

Applications:

- Electric vehicles
- DC drives
- Power supplies

---

## Types of Choppers

### Buck Chopper

$$
V_o < V_s
$$

### Boost Chopper

$$
V_o > V_s
$$

### Buck-Boost Chopper

Output can be higher or lower.

---

## Working Steps

1. Apply DC input
2. Switch ON/OFF
3. Control duty cycle
4. Obtain required output

![PE Image 11](./PE%20Image%2011.png)

---

## Important Equations

### Duty Cycle

$$
D=\frac{T_{on}}{T}
$$

### Buck Converter

$$
V_o = D \times V_s
$$

### Boost Converter

$$
V_o=\frac{V_s}{1-D}
$$

---

# Cycloconverter / AC Controller

A cycloconverter converts AC directly into AC of different voltage/frequency without intermediate DC stage.

---

## 1. AC Voltage Controller

### Features

- Controls output voltage
- Same frequency as input
- Uses SCR/TRIAC

### Applications

- Lighting control
- Heater control
- Soft starters

---

## 2. Cycloconverter

### Features

- Controls voltage and frequency
- Output frequency usually lower

### Applications

- Rolling mills
- Cement mills
- Ships

---

## Working Steps

1. Apply AC input
2. Use SCRs
3. Control firing angle
4. Obtain desired output

![PE Image 12](./PE%20Image%2012.png)

---

## Important Equations

### RMS Output Voltage

$$
V_o = V_s \sqrt{\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\pi-\alpha+\frac{\sin 2\alpha}{2}\right)}
$$

### Frequency Relation

$$
f_o < f_s
$$

---

## Important Points

- AC → AC conversion
- No intermediate DC stage
- Output frequency less than or equal to input frequency
- Controlled using firing angle:

$$
\alpha
$$

---

## Limitations

- High harmonics
- Low power factor
- Complex control
