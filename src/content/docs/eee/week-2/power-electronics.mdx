## Power Electronics

### Introduction

Power electronics is the study of conversion and control of electrical power using semiconductor devices. It is used to efficiently convert power from one form to another (AC ↔ DC, DC ↔ DC, AC ↔ AC).

Power electronics is the branch of electrical engineering that deals with:

- Conversion of electrical power
- Control of electrical power
- Conditioning of electrical power

**Steps**

- Input power supply (AC/DC)
- Use power semiconductor device
- Control switching (ON/OFF)
- Get desired output (controlled voltage/current)

**Power electronics plays a major role in:**

- Electric vehicles
- Renewable energy systems
- UPS systems
- Battery chargers
- Industrial automation
- Motor drives
- Robotics
- HVDC transmission



### A. Types of Power Conversion

- AC to DC → Rectifier
- DC to AC → Inverter
- DC to DC → Chopper
- AC to AC → Cycloconverter / AC Controller

#### 1. Switching Mode

Power semiconductor devices are operated mainly in ON and OFF states because switching operation minimizes power dissipation.

When the device is:

- **Fully ON** → Voltage drop is very small
- **Fully OFF** → Current becomes nearly zero

Hence power loss becomes minimum.

If devices operate in the linear region continuously:

- Heat generation increases
- Efficiency decreases
- Cooling requirements increase

Therefore switching mode operation is preferred in all modern power electronic systems.

#### 2. Reverse Recovery Time ($t_{rr}$)

Reverse recovery time is the time required for a diode to switch from forward conducting mode to reverse blocking mode.

During forward conduction, minority charge carriers are stored inside the junction. When reverse voltage is suddenly applied, these carriers require some time to recombine before the diode completely blocks reverse current. This interval is called reverse recovery time.

**Effects of Reverse Recovery**

- Increases switching losses
- Produces heat
- Causes EMI noise
- Reduces efficiency

**Applications**

- High-frequency converters
- SMPS
- Inverters
- Switching regulators

Fast recovery diodes and Schottky diodes are preferred in high-frequency applications because of their very small reverse recovery time.



### B. Thyristor Characteristics

A thyristor is a 4-layer (PNPN) semiconductor device acting as a high-power, unidirectional bistable switch with anode, cathode, and gate terminals. Key characteristics include Forward Blocking, Reverse Blocking, and Forward Conduction modes, allowing it to handle high voltages ($> 12\text{ kV}$) and currents ($> 6\text{ kA}$). Once triggered by a positive gate pulse, it stays conductive until the anode current drops below a holding level, requiring external commutation to turn off.

#### 1. Static Characteristics

Describes the V-I behavior during ON and OFF states.

- Forward Blocking → Forward Conduction → Reverse Blocking

#### 2. Dynamic Characteristics

Describe switching behavior:

- Turn-On Time
- Turn-Off Time
- Reverse Recovery

**Special Case**

- Devices operate in switching mode (ON/OFF)
- Reduces power loss compared to linear devices

**Important Points**

- Power electronics deals with high voltage & current
- Devices act as switches, not amplifiers
- SCR → cannot turn OFF by gate
- MOSFET → fast switching
- IGBT → high power capability

**Limitations**

- Switching losses
- Harmonics in output
- Requires proper cooling



### C. Power Semiconductor Devices

Power semiconductor devices are electronic switches used for handling high voltage and high current.

**Important Features**

- High current capability

- High efficiency
- Compact size

**Important Devices**

- Diode
- SCR

- MOSFET

#### 1. Diode

**Definition**

A diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device that allows current to flow in one direction only and blocks it in the opposite direction.

**Symbol & Terminals**

- Anode (A) → Positive terminal
- Cathode (K) → Negative terminal
- Current flows from Anode → Cathode

**Working Principle**

A diode is made from a PN junction:

- P-side → holes (positive charge carriers)
- N-side → electrons (negative charge carriers)

At the junction, a depletion region is created due to recombination of electrons and holes. This depletion region acts as a barrier preventing current flow.

##### a. Biasing of Diode

***Forward Bias***

In forward bias:

- P-side connected to positive terminal
- N-side connected to negative terminal

Effects:

- Depletion region decreases
- Barrier potential reduces
- Current flows easily

The diode behaves like a **closed switch**.

***Reverse Bias***

In reverse bias:

- P-side connected to negative terminal
- N-side connected to positive terminal

Effects:

- Depletion region widens
- Current flow is blocked
- Only small leakage current flows

The diode behaves like an **open switch**.

##### c. Breakdown Mechanisms

***Zener Breakdown***

Occurs in heavily doped diodes at low reverse voltage due to strong electric field.

***Avalanche Breakdown***

Occurs at higher reverse voltages due to collision ionization.

##### d. Types of Diodes

**a.1. PN Junction Diode**

A PN junction diode is a two-terminal semiconductor device formed by joining p-type (holes) and n-type (electrons) materials, allowing current to flow in one direction only. It operates by creating a depletion region at the junction, enabling conduction when forward-biased and blocking current when reverse-biased, serving as a rectifier.

- Applications: Rectifiers, Clippers, Clampers

**a.2. Zener Diode**

A Zener diode is a heavily doped silicon P-N junction diode designed to operate reliably in reverse bias breakdown, maintaining a constant voltage ($V_Z$) between $2.4\text{ V}$ and $200\text{ V}$. It acts as a normal diode in forward bias but allows stable, controlled current flow in reverse when the voltage exceeds its rating.

- Features: Operates in reverse bias, Maintains constant voltage
- Applications: Voltage regulators, Surge protection, Reference voltage circuits

**a.3. LED (Light Emitting Diode)**

A Light Emitting Diode (LED) is a semiconductor device that converts electrical energy into light via electroluminescence. When forward-biased, electrons in the n-type layer recombine with holes in the p-type layer at a P-N junction, releasing energy as photons.

- Advantages: Low power consumption, Long life, High efficiency
- Applications: Displays, Indicators, Lighting systems

**a.4. Photodiode**

A photodiode is a semiconductor p-n junction device that converts light (photons) into electrical current, operating primarily in reverse bias to act as a light detector or sensor. It detects ultraviolet, visible, or infrared light, generating a photocurrent proportional to the light intensity. Common materials include Silicon and Germanium.

- Applications: Optical communication, Light sensors, Automatic control systems

**a.5. Schottky Diode**

A Schottky diode is a specialized metal-semiconductor junction diode known for its very low forward voltage drop (typically $0.15\text{ V}$–$0.45\text{ V}$) and ultra-fast switching speeds. Unlike standard P-N junction diodes, they use a metal–n-type semiconductor contact, making them ideal for high-efficiency, high-frequency, and low-voltage applications such as power supplies, RF mixers, and solar cells.

- Applications: SMPS, High-frequency circuits, RF systems

**Advantages of Diode**

- Simple construction
- High reliability
- Low cost
- Fast operation

**Limitations of Diode**

- Allows current only in one direction
- Power loss due to forward voltage drop
- Reverse breakdown limitations

#### 2. SCR (Silicon Controlled Rectifier)

**Definition**

An SCR (Silicon Controlled Rectifier) is a three-terminal semiconductor device used as a controlled switch to regulate power. It allows current to flow only when triggered.

**Terminals**

- Anode (A)
- Cathode (K)
- Gate (G)

Current flows from Anode → Cathode when triggered.

**Construction**

- Made of four layers (PNPN)
- Forms three junctions: $J_1$, $J_2$, $J_3$

**Working Principle**

***Forward Blocking Mode***

- Anode positive, cathode negative
- SCR does not conduct (OFF state)
- Acts like an open switch

***Forward Conduction Mode***

- Gate pulse applied
- SCR turns ON
- Current flows freely
- Once ON → remains ON even if gate signal is removed

***Reverse Blocking Mode***

- Anode negative, cathode positive
- No conduction (like reverse-biased diode)

**V-I Characteristics**

- Initially OFF in forward bias
- Turns ON at: Gate triggering OR Breakover voltage
- After ON → low voltage drop across SCR

**Important Terms**

***Latching Current ($I_L$)***

Minimum current required to keep SCR ON after triggering.

***Holding Current ($I_H$)***

Minimum current below which SCR turns OFF.

$$I_L > I_H$$

**Switching Action**

- Turn ON → By gate pulse
- Turn OFF → Reduce current below holding current

**Two-Transistor Analogy**

SCR can be represented as two transistors (one PNP, one NPN). Positive feedback causes rapid switching.

**Advantages**

- High efficiency
- Handles high voltage & current
- Fast switching
- Compact

**Applications**

- Controlled rectifiers
- Light dimmers
- Motor speed control
- Power control circuits
- Inverters and converters

**SCR as a Switch**

- OFF state → Open switch
- ON state → Closed switch

#### 3. MOSFET

**Definition**

A MOSFET is a voltage-controlled semiconductor device used for switching and amplification. It controls current using an electric field.

**Terminals**

- Gate (G) → Control terminal
- Drain (D) → Output terminal
- Source (S) → Input terminal

Current flows from Drain → Source.

**Construction**

- Built on a semiconductor substrate
- Gate is insulated from channel by oxide layer ($\text{SiO}_2$)
- Hence very high input impedance

##### a. Types of MOSFET

***Enhancement MOSFET***

- Normally OFF
- Requires gate voltage to turn ON

***Depletion MOSFET***

- Normally ON
- Can be turned OFF using gate voltage

***Channel Types***

- N-channel MOSFET
- P-channel MOSFET

N-channel is more commonly used due to higher efficiency.

**Working Principle (N-Channel Enhancement MOSFET)**

- **Case 1:** $V_{GS} = 0$ → No channel formed, no current flows
- **Case 2:** $V_{GS} > V_T$ (Threshold Voltage) → Channel forms between drain and source, current starts flowing

**Regions of Operation**

- **Cut-off Region:** $V_{GS} < V_T$ → Device OFF
- **Linear (Ohmic) Region:** Acts like a resistor, current increases linearly
- **Saturation Region:** Acts like a switch/amplifier, current becomes constant

#### 4. IGBT (Insulated Gate Bipolar Transistor)

**Definition**

An IGBT is a three-terminal power semiconductor device that combines:

- MOSFET's high input impedance
- BJT's high current carrying capability

Used mainly in power electronics for switching and control.

**Terminals**

- Gate (G) → Control terminal
- Collector (C) → Input (from supply)
- Emitter (E) → Output

Current flows from Collector → Emitter.

**Construction**

- Combines MOSFET input structure with BJT output structure
- Has four layers (PNPN) similar to SCR but controlled like MOSFET

**Working Principle**

***OFF State:*** $V_{GE} = 0$ → No conduction, acts as an open switch

***ON State:*** Apply positive gate voltage → Channel forms (like MOSFET) → Current flows (like BJT)

**Characteristics**

$$r = \sqrt{\left(\frac{V_{rms}}{V_{dc}}\right)^2 - 1}$$



### E. Inverter

An inverter is a power electronic device that converts DC (direct current) into AC (alternating current). It is widely used in applications like UPS, solar systems, and motor drives.

**Types of Inverters**

- Single-phase inverter
- Three-phase inverter
- Voltage Source Inverter (VSI)
- Current Source Inverter (CSI)

**Steps**

- Input DC supply
- Use switching devices (MOSFET/IGBT)
- Switch ON/OFF in sequence
- Produce AC output waveform

**Important Equations**

Output Voltage (Square Wave Inverter):

$$V_o = \pm V_{dc}$$

RMS Output Voltage (Square Wave):

$$V_{rms} = V_{dc}$$

Frequency:

$$f = \frac{1}{T}$$

### F. Chopper (DC–DC Converter)

A chopper is a power electronic device that converts fixed DC into variable DC voltage. It works like a high-speed switch, widely used in electric vehicles, DC drives, and power supplies.

**Types of Choppers**

- **Step-down (Buck) Chopper** → Output < Input
- **Step-up (Boost) Chopper** → Output > Input
- **Buck-Boost Chopper** → Output can be higher or lower

**Steps**

- Apply DC input
- Switch (ON/OFF) using semiconductor device
- Control duty cycle
- Obtain required DC output

**Important Equations**

Duty Cycle:

$$D = \frac{T_{on}}{T}$$

Step-down (Buck) Chopper:

$$V_o = D \times V_s$$

Step-up (Boost) Chopper:

$$V_o = \frac{V_s}{1 - D}$$

**Special Case**

- When switch is ON → energy stored
- When switch is OFF → energy released
- Output depends on duty cycle ($D$)

**Important Points**

- Chopper = DC to DC converter
- Uses high-speed switching
- Duty cycle controls output voltage
- High efficiency compared to linear regulators
- Used in battery-powered systems

**Limitations**

- Switching noise
- Requires filters
- Complex control circuits



### G. Cycloconverter / AC Controller

A cycloconverter (AC controller) is a power electronic device that converts AC power directly into AC power of different voltage or frequency without using an intermediate DC stage. It is commonly used in speed control of AC motors.

**Types**

AC voltage controllers regulate RMS output voltage while maintaining input frequency, commonly used for lighting control and heater loads. Cycloconverters directly convert AC, altering both magnitude and frequency, often producing lower output frequencies suitable for large, low-speed motor drives. Both are crucial AC-AC converters for power electronic applications.

##### a. AC Voltage Controller

- Controls output voltage; frequency remains same as input
- Function: Converts fixed AC voltage to variable AC voltage at the same frequency
- Components: Uses thyristors (SCRs) or TRIACs for phase control
- Applications: Lighting control, heater control, soft starters

##### b. Cycloconverter

- Controls both voltage and frequency; output frequency is usually lower than input
- Function: Converts input AC (fixed voltage/frequency) directly to output AC (variable voltage/frequency)
- Types: Step-up (high freq) and Step-down (low freq)
- Applications: High-power, low-speed drives (e.g., rolling mills, cement mills), ships

**Steps**

- Apply AC input
- Use thyristors (SCRs) as switches
- Control firing angle
- Obtain required AC output

**Important Equations**

RMS Output Voltage (AC Controller):

$$V_o = V_s \sqrt{\frac{1}{\pi}\left(\pi - \alpha + \frac{\sin 2\alpha}{2}\right)}$$

Frequency Relation (Cycloconverter):

$$f_o < f_s$$

**Special Case**

- Uses SCRs in anti-parallel configuration
- Output waveform is distorted (non-sinusoidal)

### Problems

#### 1. Numerical on Half Wave and Full Wave Rectifier

**Given:** $V_m = 120\text{ V}$

**Half Wave Rectifier (HWR)**

$$V_{dc} = \frac{V_m}{\pi} = \frac{120}{3.14} = 38.2\text{ V}$$

$$V_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{2} = \frac{120}{2} = 60\text{ V}$$

**Full Wave Rectifier (FWR)**

$$V_{dc} = \frac{2V_m}{\pi} = \frac{2 \times 120}{3.14} = 76.4\text{ V}$$

$$V_{rms} = \frac{V_m}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{120}{1.414} = 84.85\text{ V}$$

#### 2. Buck Chopper Numerical

**Given:** $V_s = 240\text{ V}$, $D = 0.7$, $I_o = 5\text{ A}$

$$V_o = D \times V_s = 0.7 \times 240 = 168\text{ V}$$

$$P_o = V_o \times I_o = 168 \times 5 = 840\text{ W}$$

#### 3. SCR Numerical

**Given:** $I_L = 60\text{ mA}$, $I_H = 40\text{ mA}$

Condition: SCR remains ON if $I_A > I_H$

- $I_A = 70\text{ mA} > 40\text{ mA}$ → **SCR remains ON**
- $I_A = 30\text{ mA} < 40\text{ mA}$ → **SCR turns OFF**

#### 4. Inverter Numerical

**Given:** $V_{dc} = 230\text{ V}$, $T = 0.02\text{ s}$

$$V_{rms} = V_{dc} = 230\text{ V}$$

$$f = \frac{1}{T} = \frac{1}{0.02} = 50\text{ Hz}$$
