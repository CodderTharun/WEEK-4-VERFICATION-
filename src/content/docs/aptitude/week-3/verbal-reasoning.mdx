# Verbal Reasoning - Week 3: Argument Evaluation and Bias

## Introduction

This week advances your critical reading by teaching argument evaluation and identification of bias and logical fallacies in written material.

## Key Concepts

### 1. Argument Components

**Every argument has:**
- Claim/Conclusion: What the author is trying to prove
- Evidence/Premises: Facts or reasoning supporting the claim
- Assumptions: Unstated beliefs underlying the argument

**Structure:**
Because [Evidence] → Therefore [Conclusion]

### 2. Evaluating Arguments

**Critical Questions:**
- Is the claim clearly stated?
- Is the evidence relevant?
- Is there sufficient evidence?
- Are there unstated assumptions?
- Could alternative explanations exist?

### 3. Logical Fallacies

**Common Types:**
- **Ad Hominem**: Attacking the person instead of the argument
- **Strawman**: Misrepresenting opponent's position
- **Hasty Generalization**: Drawing conclusions from insufficient evidence
- **False Cause**: Assuming X caused Y without proof
- **Bandwagon**: Something is true because many believe it
- **Circular Reasoning**: Conclusion restates the premise

### 4. Recognizing Bias

**Author Bias Indicators:**
- Emotional language
- One-sided presentation
- Dismissal of counterarguments
- Selective evidence
- Loaded terms or phrases

**Types of Bias:**
- Personal bias
- Political bias
- Cultural bias
- Professional bias

## This Week's Topics

1. Identifying argument structure
2. Evaluating evidence quality
3. Recognizing logical fallacies
4. Detecting author bias
5. Strengthening and weakening arguments

## Practice Exercise

**Argument:** "Social media is harmful because teenagers spend too much time on it, and spending too much time on anything is bad."

**Analyze:**
1. What is the main claim?
2. What evidence is provided?
3. What assumptions are made?
4. Are there logical fallacies?
5. Could this argument be strengthened?

## Fallacy Recognition Checklist

- Does the author attack opponents rather than ideas?
- Is there evidence for causal claims?
- Are generalizations based on sufficient cases?
- Are alternative explanations considered?
- Is the argument circular?

## Tips for Success

- Read arguments actively and critically
- Question each claim and evidence
- Look for unstated assumptions
- Identify emotional language
- Practice with diverse argument types
- Learn to strengthen and weaken arguments
- Develop balanced perspective

Master argument evaluation for critical thinking!
