### A. Tests of Divisibility

##### **1. Divisibility By 2** 

A number is divisible by 2 if its unit digit is any of 0, 2, 4, 6, 8.

Ex. 58694 is divisible by 2, while 86945 is not divisible by 2.

---

##### **2. Divisibility By 3**

A number is divisible by 3 only when the sum of its digits is divisible by 3.

Ex.
(i) In the number 695421, the sum of digits = 27, which is divisible by 3.  
∴ 695421 is divisible by 3.

(ii) In the number 948653, the sum of digits = 35, which is not divisible by 3.  
∴ 948653 is not divisible by 3.

---

##### **3. Divisibility By 9**

A number is divisible by 9 only when the sum of its digits is divisible by 9.

Ex.
(i) In the number 246591, the sum of digits = 27, which is divisible by 9.  
∴ 246591 is divisible by 9.

(ii) In the number 734519, the sum of digits = 29, which is not divisible by 9.  
∴ 734519 is not divisible by 9.

---

##### **4. Divisibility By 4**

A number is divisible by 4 if the number formed by its last two digits is divisible by 4.

Ex.
(i) 6879376 is divisible by 4, since 76 is divisible by 4.

    (ii) 496138 is not divisible by 4, since 38 is not divisible by 4.

---

##### **5. Divisibility By 8**

A number is divisible by 8 if the number formed by its last three digits is divisible by 8.

Ex.
(i) In the number 16789352, the number formed by last 3 digits, namely 352 is divisible by 8.  
∴ 16789352 is divisible by 8.

(ii) In the number 576484, the number formed by last 3 digits, namely 484 is not divisible by 8.  
∴ 576484 is not divisible by 8.

---

##### **6. Divisibility By 10**

A number is divisible by 10 only when its unit digit is 0.

Ex.
(i) 7849320 is divisible by 10, since its unit digit is 0.

(ii) 678405 is not divisible by 10, since its unit digit is not 0.

---

##### **7. Divisibility By 5**

A number is divisible by 5 only when its unit digit is 0 or 5.

Ex.
(i) Each of the numbers 76895 and 68790 is divisible by 5.

---

##### **8. Divisibility By 11**

A number is divisible by 11 if the difference between the sum of its digits at odd places and the sum of its digits at even places is either 0 or a number divisible by 11.
```text
Example:
(i) Consider the number 29435417

(Sum of digits at odd places) - (Sum of digits at even places)
= (7 + 4 + 3 + 9) - (1 + 5 + 4 + 2)
= 23 - 12
= 11
Since 11 is divisible by 11,
∴ 29435417 is divisible by 11
```
```text
(ii) Consider the number 57463822

(Sum of digits at odd places) - (Sum of digits at even places)
= (2 + 8 + 6 + 7) - (2 + 3 + 4 + 5)
= 23 - 14
= 9
Since 9 is not divisible by 11,
∴ 57463822 is not divisible by 11
```

---

##### **9. Divisibility By 25**

A number is divisible by 25 if the number formed by its last two digits is either 00 or divisible by 25.

```text
Example:

(i) In the number 63875,
the number formed by the last 2 digits is 75.
75 is divisible by 25.
∴ 63875 is divisible by 25

(ii) In the number 96445,
the number formed by the last 2 digits is 45.
45 is not divisible by 25.
∴ 96445 is not divisible by 25
```

## H.C.F. and L.C.M. of Numbers

### **A. Factors and Multiples**

If a number a divides another number b exactly, we say that a is a factor of b. In this case, b is called a multiple of a.

---

### **B. Highest Common Factor (H.C.F.) or Greatest Common Measure (G.C.M.) or Greatest Common Divisor (G.C.D.)**

The H.C.F. of two or more than two numbers is the greatest number that divides each of them exactly.

There are two methods of finding the H.C.F. of a given set of numbers:

##### **1. Factorization Method**

Express each one of the given numbers as the product of prime factors.

The product of least powers of common prime factors gives H.C.F.

##### **2. Division Method**

Suppose we have to find the H.C.F. of two given numbers.
- Divide the larger number by the smaller one.
- Divide the divisor by the remainder.
- Repeat the process till remainder becomes zero.

The last divisor is the required H.C.F.

##### Finding the H.C.F. of More Than Two Numbers

Suppose we have to find the H.C.F. of three numbers.

Then,
H.C.F. of: 
[(H.C.F. of any two) and (the third number)]
gives the H.C.F. of three given numbers.
Similarly, the H.C.F. of more than three numbers may be obtained.

---

### C. Least Common Multiple (L.C.M.)

The least number which is exactly divisible by each one of the given numbers is called their L.C.M.

##### **1. Factorization Method of Finding L.C.M.**

Resolve each one of the given numbers into a product of prime factors.

Then, L.C.M. is the product of highest powers of all the factors.

##### Common Division Method (Short-cut Method) of Finding L.C.M.

- Arrange the given numbers in a row in any order.
- Divide by a number which divides exactly at least two of the given numbers.
- Carry forward the numbers which are not divisible.
- Repeat the process till no two of the numbers are divisible by the same number except 1.

The product of the divisors and the undivided numbers is the required L.C.M. of the given numbers.

---

### D. Product of Two Numbers

Product of two numbers = Product of their H.C.F. and L.C.M.

---

### E. Co-primes

Two numbers are said to be co-primes if their H.C.F. is 1.

---

### F. H.C.F. and L.C.M. of Fractions

##### H.C.F. of Fractions

$$
\text{H.C.F.} =
\frac{\text{H.C.F. of Numerators}}
{\text{L.C.M. of Denominators}}
$$

##### L.C.M. of Fractions

$$
\text{L.C.M.} =
\frac{\text{L.C.M. of Numerators}}
{\text{H.C.F. of Denominators}}
$$

---

### G. H.C.F. and L.C.M. of Decimal Fractions

In given numbers, make the same number of decimal places by annexing zeros in some numbers, if necessary.

Considering these numbers without decimal point, find H.C.F. or L.C.M. as the case may be.

Now, in the result, mark off as many decimal places as are there in each of the given numbers.

---

### H. Comparison of Fractions

Find the L.C.M. of the denominators of the given fractions.

Convert each of the fractions into an equivalent fraction with L.C.M. as the denominator, by multiplying both the numerator and denominator by the same number.

The resultant fraction with the greatest numerator is the greatest.

### I. Example Problems

```text
Ex. 1 Find the H.C.F. of:
(a) 42, 63 and 140

Solution:
42 = 2 × 3 × 7
63 = 3² × 7
140 = 2² × 5 × 7
∴ H.C.F. = 7
```
```text
(b) 108, 288 and 360

Solution:
108 = 2² × 3³
288 = 2⁵ × 3²
360 = 2³ × 5 × 3²
∴ H.C.F. = 2² × 3² = 36
```
```text
Ex. 2 Two numbers are in the ratio 3 : 4. Their L.C.M. is 84.
Find the numbers.

Solution:
Let the numbers be:
3x and 4x
Then,
L.C.M. = 12x
So,
12x = 84
x = 7
Therefore, the numbers are:
21 and 28
```
```text
Ex. 3 What is the least number which when divided by 3, 5, 6, 8, 10 and 12
leaves remainder 2 in each case, but when divided by 13 leaves no remainder?

Solution:
L.C.M. of 3, 5, 6, 8, 10 and 12 = 120
So, the required number is of the form:
120k + 2
Least value of k for which:
120k + 2
is divisible by 13 is:
k = 8
Required number:
(120 × 8) + 2 = 962
```

## Ratio and Proportion

##### **IMPORTANT CONCEPTS**

### A. Ratio

The ratio of two quantities a and b in the same units is the fraction:
$$
\frac{a}{b}
$$
written as:
$$
a : b
$$
In the ratio \(a : b\),
- a is called the antecedent (first term)
- b is called the consequent (second term)

##### Example

$$
5 : 9 = \frac{5}{9}
$$
where antecedent = 5 and consequent = 9.
##### Rule
Multiplying or dividing each term of a ratio by the same non-zero
number does not change the ratio.
##### Example

$$
4 : 5 = 8 : 10 = 12 : 15
$$

$$
4 : 6 = 2 : 3
$$

---

### B. Proportion

The equality of two ratios is called a proportion.
If
$$
a : b = c : d
$$
then we write:
$$
a : b :: c : d
$$
Here,
- a and d are called extremes
- b and c are called mean terms

##### Property

Product of means = Product of extremes
$$
b \times c = a \times d
$$
---

### C. Proportion Types

##### **1. Fourth Proportional**
If
$$
a : b = c : d
$$
<small>
then d is the fourth proportional to a, b and c.
</small>
---

##### **2. Third Proportional**

If

$$
a : b = b : c
$$

then c is the third proportional to a and b.

---

##### **3. Mean Proportional**

Mean proportional between a and b:

$$
\sqrt{ab}
$$

---

### D. Ratios

##### **1. Comparison of Ratios**

$$
\frac{a}{b} > \frac{c}{d}
\iff
ad > bc
$$

---

##### **2.Compounded Ratio**

The compounded ratio of:
$$
(a : b), (c : d), (e : f)
$$
is:
$$
(ace : bdf)
$$

---

### E. Special Ratios

##### **1. Duplicate Ratio**

Duplicate ratio of:

$$
(a : b) = (a^2 : b^2)
$$

---

##### **2. Sub-duplicate Ratio**

Sub-duplicate ratio of:

$$
(a : b) = (\sqrt{a} : \sqrt{b})
$$

---

##### **3. Triplicate Ratio**

Triplicate ratio of:

$$
(a : b) = (a^3 : b^3)
$$

---

##### **4. Sub-triplicate Ratio**

Sub-triplicate ratio of:

$$
(a : b) =
(a^{1/3} : b^{1/3})
$$

---

##### **5. Componendo and Dividendo**

If

$$
\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}
$$

then

$$
\frac{a + b}{a - b}
=
\frac{c + d}{c - d}
$$

---

### F. Variation

##### **1. Direct Variation**
$$
x \propto y
$$

$$
x = ky
$$

where \(k\) is a constant.

---

##### **2. Inverse Variation**

$$
x \propto \frac{1}{y}
$$

$$
xy = k
$$

---

### G. Replacement (Mixture Concept)

If a container has x units of liquid and y units are replaced each time, then after n operations, the quantity of pure liquid left is:

$$
x \times
\left(
1 - \frac{y}{x}
\right)^n
$$

## Averages

##### **IMPORTANT FACTS AND FORMULAE**

### A. Average Formula
$$
\text{Average}
=
\frac{\text{Sum of observations}}
{\text{Number of observations}}
$$
---

### B. Average Speed (Equal Distances)

If a man covers a certain distance at \(x\) km/h and an equal distance at \(y\) km/h, then the average speed for the whole journey is:

$$
\text{Average Speed}
=
\frac{2xy}{x + y}
\text{ km/h}
$$

### C. Example Problems
```text
Ex. 1
Find the average of all prime numbers between 30 and 50.

Solution:
Prime numbers between 30 and 50 are:
31, 37, 41, 43, 47
Number of terms = 5
Sum:
31 + 37 + 41 + 43 + 47 = 199
Required average:
199/5 = 39.8
```
```text
Ex. 2
In a class there are 50 students. Their average weight is 45 kg. 
When one student leaves the class, the average is reduced by 100 g.
Find the weight of the student who left the class.

Solution:
Total weight of 50 students:
45 × 50 = 2250 kg
New average of 49 students:
45 - 0.1 = 44.9 kg
Total weight of 49 students:
44.9 × 49 = 2200.1 kg
Weight of the student who left:
2250 - 2200.1 = 49.9 kg
```

#### Profit and Loss Formula Table


| Concept | Formula / Definition |
|---|---|
| **Cost Price (C.P.)** | The price at which an article is purchased. |
| **Selling Price (S.P.)** | The price at which an article is sold. |
| **Profit / Gain** | $S.P. > C.P.$ |
| **Loss** | $S.P. < C.P.$ |
| **Gain** | $\text{Gain} = S.P - C.P$ |
| **Loss Formula** | $\text{Loss} = C.P - S.P$ |
| **Gain%** | $\text{Gain\%} = \left(\frac{\text{Gain}}{\text{C.P.}}\right)\times100$ |
| **Loss%** | $\text{Loss\%} = \left(\frac{\text{Loss}}{\text{C.P.}}\right)\times100$ |
| **Selling Price Formula (Gain)** | $S.P = \left(\frac{100+\text{Gain\%}}{100}\right)\times C.P$ |
| **Selling Price Formula (Loss)** | $S.P = \left(\frac{100-\text{Loss\%}}{100}\right)\times C.P$ |
| **Cost Price Formula (Gain)** | $C.P = \left(\frac{100}{100+\text{Gain\%}}\right)\times S.P$ |
| **Cost Price Formula (Loss)** | $C.P = \left(\frac{100}{100-\text{Loss\%}}\right)\times S.P$ |
| **If Gain is $x\%$** | $S.P = (100+x)\% \text{ of C.P.}$ |
| **If Loss is $x\%$** | $S.P = (100-x)\% \text{ of C.P.}$ |
| **Equal Gain and Loss on Similar Items** | $\text{Net Loss\%} = \frac{x^2}{100}$ |
| **False Weight at Cost Price** | $\text{Gain\%} = \left(\frac{\text{Error}}{\text{True Value}-\text{Error}}\right)\times100$ |
| **Claimed $x\%$ Profit with $y\%$ Less Weight** | $\text{Gain\%} = \left(\frac{x+y}{100-y}\right)\times100$ |
| **Claimed $x\%$ Loss with $y\%$ Less Weight** | $\text{Net Gain/Loss\%} = \left(\frac{y-x}{100-y}\right)\times100$ |
| **Result Interpretation** | Positive Result → Gain <br /> Negative Result → Loss |

#### Simple and Compound Interest Table


| Concept | Formula / Definition |
|---|---|
| **Principal (P)** | The money borrowed or lent for a certain period is called the principal. |
| **Interest** | The extra money paid for using someone else’s money is called interest. |
| **Simple Interest (S.I.)** | If interest is calculated uniformly on the principal for the entire time, it is called simple interest.<br /><br />Let:<br />$P$ = Principal <br />$R$ = Rate of interest per annum (%) <br />$T$ = Time (in years) |
| **Simple Interest Formula** | $\text{S.I} = \frac{P \times R \times T}{100}$ |
| **Principal Formula** | $P = \frac{\text{S.I} \times 100}{R \times T}$ |
| **Rate Formula** | $R = \frac{\text{S.I} \times 100}{P \times T}$ |
| **Time Formula** | $T = \frac{\text{S.I} \times 100}{P \times R}$ |
| **Amount** | $A = P + \text{S.I}$ |
| **Compound Interest** | Let:<br />$P$ = Principal <br />$R$ = Rate of interest per annum (%) <br />$n$ = Time (in years) |
| **Compounded Annually** | $A = P\left(1+\frac{R}{100}\right)^n$ |
| **Compounded Half-Yearly** | $A = P\left(1+\frac{(R/2)}{100}\right)^{2n}$ |
| **Compounded Quarterly** | $A = P\left(1+\frac{(R/4)}{100}\right)^{4n}$ |
| **Fractional Time** | When interest is compounded annually but time is fractional.<br /><br />$A = P\left(1+\frac{R}{100}\right)^3\left(1+\frac{(R/2)}{100}\right)$ |
| **Different Rates for Different Years** | If rates are:<br />$R_1\%$, $R_2\%$, $R_3\%$<br /><br />$A = P\left(1+\frac{R_1}{100}\right)\left(1+\frac{R_2}{100}\right)\left(1+\frac{R_3}{100}\right)$ |
| **Present Worth** | Present Worth ($P$) of ₹x due after n years:<br /><br />$P = \frac{x}{\left(1+\frac{R}{100}\right)^n}$ |

### B. Example Problems
```text
Ex. 1
A certain sum of money becomes three times of itself in 
20 years at simple interest.In how many years does it become
double of itself at the same rate?

Solution:
Let Principal = P
When amount becomes 3P,
S.I. = 3P - P = 2P
Time:
T = 20 years
Using S.I. formula:
S.I.=(P×R×T)/100
2P=(P×R×20)/100
R=(2×100)/20
=10%
Now for amount to become 2P,
S.I. = 2P - P = P
Using:
T=(S.I.×100)/(P×R)
T=(P×100)/(P×10)
=10 years

Therefore, required time = 10 years.
```

## Time & Work

1. If A can do a piece of work in $n$ days, then A’s 1 day work is:

$$
\frac{1}{n}
$$

2. If A’s 1 day work is:

$$
\frac{1}{n}
$$

then A can finish the work in $n$ days.

3. If A is thrice as good a workman as B, then:

- Ratio of work done by A and B:

$$
3 : 1
$$

- Ratio of time taken by A and B:

$$
1 : 3
$$



#### Example Problems

```

EX 1:

A and B together can complete a piece of work in 15 days and B alone in 20 days.
In how many days can A alone complete the work?

Solution:
A + B’s 1 day work = 1/15
B’s 1 day work = 1/20
A’s 1 day work = 1/15 − 1/20 = (4 − 3)/60 = 1/60

Therefore, A alone can complete the work in 60 days.


Ex.2 Rahul takes twice as much time as Manick and thrice as much time as Sachin to 
complete a job. If working together, they can complete the job in 4 days,
find the time taken by each of them separately to complete the work.

Solution:
Suppose Rahul takes x hours to complete the job.
Then,
Manick takes x/2 hours to complete the job.
Sachin takes x/3 hours to complete the job.
Rahul’s 1 hour work = 1/x
```

### A.Pipes and Cisterns

#### 1. Definitions

- **Inlet:**  
A pipe connected with a tank, cistern, or reservoir that fills it is known as an inlet.

- **Outlet:**  
A pipe connected with a tank, cistern, or reservoir that empties it is known as an outlet.

---

#### 2. Important Formulas

1. If a pipe can fill a tank in $x$ hours, then part filled in 1 hour is:

$$
\frac{1}{x}
$$

2. If a pipe can empty a full tank in $y$ hours, then part emptied in 1 hour is:

$$
\frac{1}{y}
$$

3. If a pipe can fill a tank in $x$ hours and another pipe can empty the full tank in $y$ hours where $y > x$, then on opening both the pipes, the net part filled in 1 hour is:

$$
\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{y}
$$

4. If a pipe can fill a tank in $x$ hours and another pipe can empty the full tank in $y$ hours where $x > y$, then on opening both the pipes, the net part emptied in 1 hour is:

$$
\frac{1}{y} - \frac{1}{x}
$$

#### 3. Example Problems
```

Ex. 1:

A tank 9 ft by 5 ft by 2 ft is fitted with an inlet pipe and an exhaust pipe.
The inlet pipe pours in 576 cu. inch of water per minute and the exhaust pipe can
empty the full tank in 3 hours. If the tank is full and both pipes are open, how
many hours will it take to empty it?


Solution:
Volume of the tank = (9 × 5 × 2) cu. ft = 90 cu. Ft.
= (90 × 12 × 12 × 12) cu. inches
Volume of water drained by the exhaust pipe in one minute
= (90 × 12 × 12 × 12) / (3 × 60)
= 864 cu. inches
Net volume drained in one minute when both pipes are opened
= (864 − 576) cu. Inches
= 288 cu. Inches
Required time
= (90 × 12 × 12 × 12) / (288 × 60) hours
= 9 hours.

Ex 2. Two pipes can fill a cistern in 14 hours and 16 hours respectively. 
The pipes are opened simultaneously and it is found that due to leakage in 
the bottom it took 32 minutes more to fill the cistern. When the cistern is full,
in what time will the leak empty it?

Solution:
Work done by the two pipes in 1 hour
= (1/14 + 1/16)
= (8 + 7)/112
= 15/112
Time taken by these pipes to fill the tank
= 112/15 hours
= 7 hours 28 minutes
Due to leakage, time taken
= 7 hours 28 minutes + 32 minutes
= 8 hours
Work done by (two pipes + leak) in 1 hour
= ⅛
Work done by the leak in 1 hour
= (15/112 − 1/8)
= (15/112 − 14/112)
= 1/112
Therefore, the leak alone can empty the full cistern in 112 hours.
```

### B. Work and Wages

#### 1. Basic Work Formula

If a person can complete a work in $n$ days, then:

$$
\text{1 day’s work} = \frac{1}{n}
$$

If 1 day’s work is:

$$
\frac{1}{n}
$$

then total work will be completed in $n$ days.


#### 2. Efficiency and Time Relation

Efficiency is inversely proportional to time.

If A is twice as efficient as B:

- Work ratio:

$$
2 : 1
$$

- Time ratio:

$$
1 : 2
$$

If efficiency ratio of A and B is:

$$
m : n
$$

then time taken ratio is:

$$
n : m
$$


#### 3. Combined Work

If A can do a work in $x$ days and B can do the same work in $y$ days, then:

A’s 1 day work:

$$
\frac{1}{x}
$$

B’s 1 day work:

$$
\frac{1}{y}
$$

Combined 1 day work:

$$
\frac{1}{x} + \frac{1}{y}
$$

Time taken together:

$$
\frac{xy}{x+y}
$$


#### 4. Difference in Work

If A can do a work in $x$ days and B in $y$ days:

$$
\left( \frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{y} \right)
$$


#### 5. Wages Formula

Wages are distributed according to work done.

If wages are proportional to efficiency:

$$
\text{Wages Ratio} = \text{Efficiency Ratio}
$$

Example:

If A and B work in the ratio:

$$
3 : 2
$$

then wages are divided in the ratio:

$$
3 : 2
$$


#### 6. Work and Time Relation

- More workers → Less time
- Less workers → More time

$$
(M_1 \times D_1 \times H_1 \times E_1)
=
(M_2 \times D_2 \times H_2 \times E_2)
$$

Where:

- $M$ = Number of men
- $D$ = Number of days
- $H$ = Working hours per day
- $E$ = Efficiency


#### 7. Net Work Formula

When both inlet and outlet are opened:

$$
\frac{1}{x} - \frac{1}{y}
$$

```
Ex 1:
A can do a piece of work in 12 days and B can do the same work in 18 days.

Solution
A’s 1 day work = 1/12
B’s 1 day work = 1/18
Ratio of work done by A and B

= 18 : 12
```
