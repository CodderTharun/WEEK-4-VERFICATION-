### Question 1

**x is a positive integer such that x² + 12 is exactly divisible by x. Find all the possible values of x.**

- A) 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12
- B) 1, 2, 4, 8, 12
- C) 2, 3, 5, 6, 12
- D) 1, 3, 6, 9, 12

**Correct Answer:** A) 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12

**Explanation:**  
Given: (x² + 12) is exactly divisible by x.  
⇒ (x² + 12) / x is an integer  
⇒ x²/x + 12/x is an integer  
⇒ x + 12/x is an integer.

Since x is already an integer, 12/x must also be an integer.

Therefore, x must be a divisor of 12.

The positive divisors of 12 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.

Hence, the possible values of x are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12.

- B) 3

7⁴ gives unit digit 1, therefore 7¹⁵² gives unit digit 1, hence 7¹⁵³ gives unit digit 1 × 7 = 7.

Also, 1⁷² gives unit digit 1.

Therefore, the unit digit in the product is: 7 × 1 = 7.

```text
(H.C.F. of numerators) / (L.C.M. of denominators)
```

```text
(L.C.M. of numerators) / (H.C.F. of denominators)
```

H.C.F. of numerators (2, 8, 16, 10) = 2.

L.C.M. of denominators (3, 9, 81, 27) = 81.

Therefore:

```text
H.C.F. = 2/81
```

L.C.M. of numerators (2, 8, 16, 10) = 80.

H.C.F. of denominators (3, 9, 81, 27) = 3.

Therefore:

```text
L.C.M. = 80/3
```

### Question 16

**In an ER model, you have entities Student, Course, and Enrollment, where Enrollment captures attributes like grade and enrollment_date. A new requirement states that a student may enroll in the same course multiple times across different semesters and each Enrollment must be uniquely identifiable, even if the same student repeats the same course in the same semester due to withdrawal and re-enrollment. Which design best preserves normalization and captures this requirement?**

A) Model Enrollment as a regular entity with a surrogate primary key `enrollment_id`, and enforce uniqueness on `(student_id, course_id, semester, attempt_number)` via a unique constraint.  

B) Merge Student and Enrollment into a single entity to avoid composite keys and track attempts as a repeating multivalued attribute `attempts` per Course.  

C) Model Enrollment as a weak entity with a composite key `(student_id, course_id, semester)` and add a surrogate key `enrollment_id` only as a non-key attribute.  

D) Model Enrollment as a ternary relationship between Student, Course and Semester with primary key `(student_id, course_id, semester_id)` and store `attempt_number` in Student.  

**Correct Answer: A) Model Enrollment as a regular entity with a surrogate primary key `enrollment_id`, and enforce uniqueness on `(student_id, course_id, semester, attempt_number)` via a unique constraint.**

**Explanation:**  
This design uses Enrollment as a regular entity with a surrogate primary key `enrollment_id` while imposing a unique constraint on `(student_id, course_id, semester, attempt_number)`. The surrogate key ensures that each enrollment row is uniquely and stably identifiable, even if business rules around attempts or semesters change over time. The unique constraint enforces that, for a given student, course and semester, each `attempt_number` is distinct, allowing multiple enrollments in the same semester while disallowing exact duplicates. This approach preserves normalization because Enrollment remains a separate entity capturing its own attributes (`grade`, `enrollment_date`, `status`, etc.), avoids redundant data and does not rely on composite natural keys that cannot express repeated attempts.

### Question 45

**In CRC error detection, how does the receiver confirm that received data is error-free?**

B) By checking if the division of received data by the divisor gives zero remainder  
C) By recalculating the checksum and comparing it with the sent checksum  
D) By counting the number of 1s in the received data  

**Correct Answer: B) By checking if the division of received data by the divisor gives zero remainder**

**Explanation:**  
CRC works on binary division. The sender appends CRC bits so the data is exactly divisible by a predetermined divisor. At the receiver's end, the same division is performed — if the remainder is zero, data is accepted as correct; any non-zero remainder means the data was corrupted in transit.
